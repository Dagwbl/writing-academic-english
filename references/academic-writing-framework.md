# Academic Writing Framework

This reference operationalizes the main logic of Oshima and Hogue's *Writing Academic English* without copying textbook prose, exercises, or examples. Use it to revise, diagnose, draft, or prompt AI for academic English.

## Diagnostic Ladder

Apply the book's global-to-local logic:

1. **Process**: Has the writer created ideas, organized them, drafted, revised, and edited?
2. **Paragraph**: Does each paragraph have a clear topic sentence, one controlling idea, support, and optional closure?
3. **Unity**: Does every sentence serve the controlling idea?
4. **Coherence**: Do sentences and paragraphs hold together through key terms, pronouns, transitions, and logical order?
5. **Support**: Are claims developed with examples, facts, statistics, quotations, paraphrases, summaries, or reasoning?
6. **Essay**: Does the introduction lead to a thesis, do body paragraphs follow a pattern, and does the conclusion synthesize?
7. **Source use**: Are borrowed words and ideas quoted, paraphrased, summarized, and cited correctly?
8. **Sentence control**: Are clause patterns, connectors, punctuation, and sentence boundaries correct?
9. **Style**: Is the prose formal, precise, concise, and readable?

## Writing Process Engine

Use this process when drafting or doing developmental feedback:

1. **Creating / prewriting**
   - Narrow broad topics into usable topics.
   - Generate ideas through listing, freewriting, clustering, questions, or source notes.
   - Separate potentially useful material from irrelevant material.

2. **Planning / outlining**
   - Group ideas under main supporting points.
   - Choose an organization pattern before drafting.
   - Build a paragraph outline with topic sentence, main supports, and details.
   - Build an essay outline with thesis, body paragraph roles, support, and conclusion.

3. **Writing / rough draft**
   - Follow the outline but allow useful new ideas.
   - Do not demand perfect grammar in the first draft.
   - Keep the controlling idea visible while drafting.

4. **Polishing**
   - Revise first: logic, unity, support, coherence, organization.
   - Edit second: sentence boundaries, clause structure, punctuation, word form, style.
   - Proofread last for surface errors.

For feedback, identify which process stage the user is stuck in. A weak draft may need planning rather than polishing.

## Paragraph Architecture

A strong academic paragraph normally contains:

- **Topic sentence**: a complete sentence naming the topic and controlling idea.
- **Supporting sentences**: explanations, examples, facts, statistics, quotations, or analysis that develop the topic sentence.
- **Concluding sentence**: optional but useful in standalone or long paragraphs; it may restate the main point, summarize key support, or close the paragraph's logic.

### Topic Sentence Checks

Use these checks:

- It is a complete sentence, not a title or fragment.
- It contains both topic and controlling idea.
- It is general enough to cover the paragraph but specific enough to guide it.
- It does not contain unrelated controlling ideas.
- It usually appears at the beginning, but may appear later when the paragraph builds inductively.

Weak pattern: "The effects of social media."

Stronger pattern: "Social media can intensify political polarization by rewarding emotionally charged content."

### Supporting Sentence Checks

Each supporting sentence should:

- explain or prove the topic sentence
- add specific detail rather than repeat the same idea
- use examples or evidence where claims need support
- stay within the controlling idea

Remove or move sentences that introduce a second main idea.

## Unity and Coherence

### Unity

Unity has two parts:

- The paragraph discusses one main idea.
- Every supporting sentence directly develops that idea.

Revision moves:

- Delete off-topic sentences.
- Split paragraphs with two controlling ideas.
- Add missing support where a topic sentence is asserted but not developed.
- Rewrite the topic sentence if the actual paragraph focus is narrower or different.

### Coherence

Coherence means the reader can follow the path of ideas. Use four main tools:

1. **Repeat key nouns or terms** when synonym-swapping would blur meaning.
2. **Use consistent pronouns** with clear antecedents.
3. **Use transition signals** to show addition, contrast, cause/effect, example, time, result, concession, summary, or conclusion.
4. **Arrange ideas in logical order**: chronological order, logical division, comparison/contrast, cause/effect, or another defensible sequence.

Do not overload prose with transition signals. If every sentence needs a connector, reorder the ideas or strengthen the paragraph logic.

## Supporting Details and Source Material

Academic support may include:

- examples
- facts
- statistics
- direct quotations
- indirect quotations / reported speech
- paraphrases
- summaries
- definitions
- explanation and reasoning

### Fact vs. Opinion

Treat opinions as claims that need support. Treat facts as support only when they are verifiable and relevant. Some factual claims still need sources.

### Using Quotations and Statistics

Use quotations sparingly and only when exact wording matters or the source's authority is important. Use statistics only with context, source attribution, and interpretation.

Always:

- introduce the source or speaker
- give the borrowed material
- explain how it supports the paragraph's controlling idea
- include citation details when available

Do not drop quotations, statistics, or paraphrases into a paragraph without analysis.

## Essay Architecture

An essay extends paragraph logic.

### Introduction

A good academic introduction usually:

1. Establishes the topic or context.
2. Narrows the focus through a problem, question, tension, debate, history, example, or surprising fact.
3. Ends or culminates in a thesis statement.

Two useful introduction shapes:

- **Funnel**: broad context -> narrowed context -> thesis.
- **Attention-opening**: brief striking fact, question, contrast, or example -> context -> thesis.

Avoid vague global openings, dictionary definitions, and unsupported claims of importance.

### Thesis Statement

The thesis is the essay-level controlling idea. It should:

- state the main idea clearly
- be specific enough to guide the body
- be limited enough to support
- often preview subtopics or organization
- sometimes signal the pattern: logical division, chronological process, cause/effect, comparison/contrast, or argument

Common pitfalls:

- too broad
- simple fact rather than claim
- announcement only: "This essay will discuss..."
- unclear controlling idea
- unrelated subtopics
- position hidden in vague wording

### Body Paragraphs

Each body paragraph should work like a paragraph-level unit:

- topic sentence connected to thesis
- one paragraph role
- specific support
- analysis after evidence
- transition to the next role when needed

### Conclusion

A conclusion may:

- paraphrase the thesis
- summarize main points
- give a final implication, warning, recommendation, or broader significance

Do not introduce major new evidence in the conclusion.

## Organization Patterns

Choose the pattern that fits the rhetorical task.

### Logical Division

Use when dividing one topic into categories, reasons, features, advantages, problems, or aspects.

Structure:

- thesis names or implies the divisions
- each body paragraph develops one division
- transitions mark order and relationship

### Chronological / Process

Use for events, history, biography, instructions, procedures, or process explanation.

Keys:

- group steps or events where natural breaks occur
- make the thesis signal time order or process
- use time-order signals accurately
- clarify whether steps are sequential, simultaneous, repeated, or conditional

### Cause / Effect

Use when explaining reasons, results, consequences, or causal chains.

Patterns:

- **Block organization**: causes together, effects together, or one major section followed by another.
- **Chain organization**: cause -> effect that becomes another cause -> next effect.
- **Combined organization**: block for larger sections and chain inside paragraphs.

Always distinguish cause from correlation when evidence is limited.

### Comparison / Contrast

Use when explaining similarities and differences.

Patterns:

- **Point-by-point**: compare subjects one criterion at a time; best for analytical comparison.
- **Block**: discuss all of subject A, then all of subject B; best when the comparison is simpler or each subject needs context.

Choose clear points of comparison. Do not list random similarities and differences.

### Argumentative Essay

Use when taking a position on an issue.

Core elements:

- clear position thesis
- support for the writer's position
- summary of opposing arguments
- rebuttal or counterargument
- evidence from sources when available
- contrast transitions connecting opposing views and rebuttals

Patterns:

- **Block**: opposing arguments in one section, rebuttals and writer's arguments in another.
- **Point-by-point**: one opposing argument followed immediately by rebuttal, repeated by point.

The thesis may acknowledge the opposing view while placing the writer's position in the main clause.

## Paraphrase, Summary, and Plagiarism Control

Use this distinction:

- **Quotation**: exact wording, quotation marks or block format, citation.
- **Paraphrase**: restates nearly all relevant meaning in new words and structure, usually similar length, citation.
- **Summary**: condenses main ideas, much shorter than the source, citation.

Avoid plagiarism by:

- citing borrowed ideas and information
- marking exact copied words as quotations
- changing both wording and sentence structure in paraphrases
- writing from notes rather than looking at the source sentence by sentence
- comparing the result with the source to ensure meaning is accurate but wording is independent

When polishing user text, flag source-use risk if a passage sounds copied, lacks citation, or uses data without attribution.

## Sentence Structure and Style

Use sentence variety to express relationships accurately.

### Sentence Types

- **Simple**: one independent clause. Useful for clarity and emphasis.
- **Compound**: two independent clauses joined by coordinator, conjunctive adverb, or semicolon. Use when ideas are equal.
- **Complex**: independent clause plus dependent clause. Use when one idea depends on or qualifies another.
- **Compound-complex**: at least two independent clauses plus at least one dependent clause. Use sparingly for complex relationships.

Choose structure based on meaning:

- equal ideas -> coordination
- unequal ideas -> subordination
- sequence -> time clauses or chronological signals
- reason/result -> cause/effect clauses or signals
- contrast -> concession or direct-opposition structures

### Clause Tools

Use noun clauses for embedded statements, questions, and reported speech.

Use adverb clauses for time, place, reason, result, purpose, condition, concession, and contrast.

Use adjective clauses to define or add information about nouns. Punctuate restrictive clauses without commas and nonrestrictive clauses with commas.

Use participial phrases to combine related information and improve style, but only when the implied subject is clear.

### Parallelism

Make parallel ideas grammatically parallel, especially in lists, comparisons, contrasts, paired conjunctions, and coordinated structures.

## Sentence Problem Repair

### Fragments

A complete sentence needs an independent clause. Repair fragments by:

- attaching the fragment to a nearby independent clause
- removing the subordinator when appropriate
- adding the missing subject or verb

### Choppy Sentences

Choppy writing overuses short simple sentences. Combine sentences when the relationship is clear:

- equal ideas -> compound sentence
- dependent idea -> complex sentence
- descriptive detail -> adjective clause or participial phrase

### Run-Ons and Comma Splices

Repair incorrectly joined independent clauses by:

- separating them into two sentences
- adding a semicolon
- adding a coordinator with correct punctuation
- adding a subordinator to make one clause dependent

### Stringy Sentences

Stringy sentences chain too many clauses, often with repeated "and," "but," or "so." Repair by splitting, subordinating, or grouping ideas into clearer units.

## Punctuation and Documentation

### Punctuation Priorities

Check:

- commas after introductory elements when needed
- commas before coordinators joining independent clauses
- commas around interrupters and nonrestrictive clauses
- semicolons between closely related independent clauses
- semicolons before conjunctive adverbs joining independent clauses
- colons before lists, appositives, explanations, or long quotations when the setup is a complete clause
- quotation marks around exact short quotations
- block formatting for long quotations when the target style requires it

### Documentation

Use the citation style requested by the user or assignment. If no style is given, keep citation advice generic.

Never fabricate bibliographic details. If source information is missing, write `[citation needed]`, `[source details needed]`, or ask for the missing information.

## Output Patterns

### Revision Output

Use:

1. **Revised version**
2. **Why it is stronger**
3. **Remaining issues**

For short requests, omit sections 2 and 3 unless useful.

### Diagnosis Output

Use a compact table:

| Level | Issue | Why it matters | Revision move |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |

Levels: thesis/topic sentence, unity, support, coherence, organization, source use, sentence structure.

### Teaching Output

Use:

- rule
- original problem
- improved version
- reusable pattern

### AI Prompt Output

Include:

- task and genre
- audience and discipline
- thesis or topic sentence
- organization pattern
- paragraph plan
- evidence and citation constraints
- tone and style requirements
- no fabrication rule
- requested output format
